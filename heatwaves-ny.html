New York Is Heating Up: What's Going On?

July 19, 2026

If you live in New York, you've probably noticed that our summers have been feeling hotter than ever. It isn't just that we have a few hot days anymore—it seems like every year, more heat waves last longer and make it harder to spend time outside.

In June 2025, New York City experienced its first major heat wave of the summer. Temperatures reached the upper 90s, but with the humidity, it felt even hotter—over 100°F in some places. Because of the dangerous conditions, New York City opened cooling centers, activated its Heat Emergency Plan, and encouraged people to stay hydrated and avoid spending too much time outdoors.

But what exactly is a heat wave, and why does it seem like we're having so many of them?

A heat wave is a period of unusually hot weather that lasts for several days. Around New York, it's generally considered a heat wave when temperatures reach 90°F or higher for at least three days in a row. Humidity also plays a big role. When there's a lot of moisture in the air, our sweat doesn't evaporate as easily, making it much harder for our bodies to cool down. That's why the "feels like" temperature, called the heat index, can be much higher than the actual air temperature.

One reason cities like New York get so hot is something called the urban heat island effect. Buildings, roads, sidewalks, and parking lots absorb heat from the sun during the day and slowly release it at night. Unlike forests or grassy areas, these surfaces don't cool down very quickly. That means cities often stay much warmer than nearby rural areas, especially after the sun goes down.

Extreme heat isn't just uncomfortable—it can also be dangerous. Heat can lead to heat exhaustion or even heat stroke, and it can be especially harmful for older adults, young children, people with medical conditions, and anyone who has to work outside. Even healthy people can become sick if they spend too much time in the heat without drinking enough water or taking breaks in the shade.

So why are heat waves becoming more common?

Scientists have found that climate change is making heat waves happen more often, last longer, and become more intense. As greenhouse gases continue to trap heat in Earth's atmosphere, average temperatures are rising. That doesn't mean every hot day is caused by climate change, but it does mean that extreme heat is becoming more likely.

I think that's one of the biggest reasons it's important to learn about climate change. Sometimes it can feel like it's a problem happening somewhere far away, but heat waves show us that it's happening right here, where we live. Whether it's a park that's too hot to enjoy, a sports practice that gets canceled, or a family staying inside because it's unsafe to be outdoors, climate change affects our everyday lives.

There are things we can do to stay safe during heat waves, like drinking plenty of water, staying in the shade or air conditioning when possible, and checking on neighbors or family members who may be more vulnerable. Looking ahead, planting more trees, creating more green spaces, and reducing greenhouse gas emissions can also help make our communities cooler and more resilient.

This is one of the reasons I wanted to start this blog. Environmental issues can sometimes seem overwhelming, but learning about them is the first step toward making a difference. Heat waves are becoming a bigger part of life in New York, but by understanding why they're happening and what we can do, we can all play a small role in creating a healthier future.
